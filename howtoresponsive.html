How To Use The Tools


The Transaction Tracker

The first tool requires the user to enter the number trade it is for them and the ticker symbol. Next, the user must enter the market value of the trade they enter and exit. This is the total cost and sale respectively. It is the number of shares times the share price that the stock was bought and sold for. The P/L will be automatically calculated based on the entry and exit values entered. If the trade was profitable (the "exit" is greater than the "enter") the text will be colored green. If the trade was sold for a loss, the text color will be red. Next, the percent gain or loss will be automatically calculated. The next column of the table asks for the number of days the user held the trade for. Finally, the "portfolio value" is automatically calculated by adding the P/L from the previous trade. This value starts at zero because it will track the total number of dollars the trader gains or loses over time. To the right of the table, there is a chart that plots the portfolio value with respect to the trade number. It is recommended to see where there are large jumps up and down to see the trades that did really well or really poorly. If it did well, you know to continue the same method, but if there are any large drops, the trader can analyze what went wrong and prevent that situation from arising again. The goal is to keep this plot trending over the red line indicating you are profitable and without a lot of volatility, or large swings up and down. The other values of this table will be used in the second part of the tool.


The Overview Of Your Transaction Tracker

The percent gains and losses calculated in the Transaction Tracker are averaged to show the user what their average gain is for when they are right, and their average loss for when they are wrong. The goal here is to have a larger average gain than average loss. Ideally, the user would want their average gain at least two times larger than their average loss in order to achieve excellent returns. Next, the user's batting average is calculated. This was a term used by Mark Minervini, and is the percent of profitable trades with respection to all of your trades. It is the total number of green trades divided by the total number of trades to tell the user what percentage of their trades are profitable. The goal is to have this value over 50%, but if the user's losses are limited well, a batting average of 40% can still allow for excellent performance. The user's gain-to-loss ratio is calculated next. This is the average percent gain divided by the average percent loss. If this value is less than 1, then the user's losses are larger than their gains. If the value is greater that 1, then the user's gains are larger than their losses. The goal should be to make this value at least 2. This means their average gain is at least two times larger than their average loss. This combined with a good batting average can allow the user to acheive an outstanding trading performance. The average profitable days held and average lossing trade days held shows the user the average number of days they hold a winning and losing trade. Finally, the largest gaining trade and largest losing trade will appear in the table. This is to just show the user, what their most profitable trade and their biggest loss was.


The Monthly Tracker

The Monthly Tracker tracks the users average gain, loss, batting average, largest profitable trade, and largest losing trade over the course of each month. The table also asks for the total number of trades the user performed in a given month. This will tell the trader if they are over trading. The Monthly Tracker also tracks the user's average gain and loss per day. This is the average gain and loss divided by the number of days in the month respectively. This will show the user how much they roughly earn and lose per day. The goal is to earn more than they lose per day with their average day gain greater than their average day loss. Finally, the total portfolio value at the end of the month is tracked here. The goal is to have each month's total value greater than the month's before indicating portfolio growth. To the right of the table is a histogram plot of the total portfolio value with respect to each month for the user to visualize their portfolio's growth. At the bottom is a smaller table to calculate the average of each month's batting average, the average of each month's average percent gain, the average of each month's average percent loss, and the user's total average gain-to-loss ratio. These averages will show the user their yearly batting average, yearly average percent gain, yearly average percent loss, and yearly gain-to-loss ratio.


The Gaussian Distribution

The Gaussian Distribution is a histogram plot. The x-axis ranges from -40% to 40% and is incremented by 2% across. The y-axis shows the total number of trades for a given percent range. For example, if the user has 5 trades between 2% and 4%, then a bar with a length of 5 will be plotted at 2%. The user should decide on a hard stop loss to use for their trading. This value will act as a wall on the left side of the distribution where no trade will ever go lower. For example, if the user's hard stop is 7%, then there will be a bar at the -6% location and no bars to the left of it. This bar is a wall. The goal is for the user to have their distribution of trades shifted to the right. The peak of their plot may be near the 0-4% range, but they will have trades over 10% and 20% to create bars to the right of their peak with no wall limiting their gains. There are two zeros on the plot. The left 0 is for trades that range between 0% and -2%, and the 0 on the right is for trades that range between 0% and 2%. The table has a column for each week. The user needs to enter each trade in its correct range and update the number throughout the week. For example if the trader has two 5% trades in a week, the need to enter a 2 in the 4% row for that week. The total number of trades for each percent range is automatically calculated at the far right of this table. At the bottom, there is a total sum of the total number of trades the user makes over time.


The Market Tracker

The last part of this tool is the Market Tracker. The user needs to enter their total portfolio value every day as well as the closing price of the SPX or other index they want to compare their trading to. The P/L for the portfolio and index is calculated for each day. There are two plots to the right of the table. The first plot shows the user's portfolio percent P/L in red with respect to the index's percent P/L in white. The goal for the user is to keep their portfolio trending over the zero line and the white line. This will indicate that they are profitable and beating the market. The second, lower plot shows the user their total portfolio value after each trading day. The goal for the user is to keep this value over the starting value to indicate profitability.
